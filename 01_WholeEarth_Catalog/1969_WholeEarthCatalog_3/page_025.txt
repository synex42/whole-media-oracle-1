Roland Jacopetti Reports 
| feel I’ve read as much of BACK TO EDEN as is safe for 
Jethro Kloss is certainly a loremaster, no mistake about 
that. He is also a very, very grim man, of the type of 
stern Christian countryfolk which were apparently 
endemic to the Midwest c. 1800-1930 and, one presumes, 
still being produced according to the original pattern in 
more limited quantities. 
i 
I’m sure there’s much in this book that’s really valuable. 
Jethro certainly knows herbs sideways and the long 
section on what all the herbs are and what they do would 
seem to be a pretty good starting point for people who 
are sick of Excedrin and penicillin and feel, | think 
rightly, that somewhere there’s some nondescript weed 
which will cure just about any ailment that might 
trouble you. If you've ever looked at the Nature’s Herb 
Co.catalog and wondered what in the world people use 
ali that stuff for, Jethro has all the answers. He does have 
some good stuff to say.about soils and farming, but 
certainly there’s lots better stuff on the subject. 
Jethro is a vegetable man, to the extent that he really 
hates pretty much everything that’s either mineral or 
animal. He doesn’t think one should eat anything except 
vegetables, fruits, and nuts, 
But he’s so damn gloomy 
about it. George Ohsawa says pretty much the same 
things, except that there’s a certain joy in his writings, 
all that nice yin-yang rap and how you can screw your 
old lady nonstop behind brown rice. Jethro mumbles 
dire warnings about prolapsed colons and tenders happy 
descriptions of unfortunate meat and Coca-Cola 
eaters whose insides have turned to scum and brown 
dreck, And anyway he’s just dying to be eating 
Hostess Twinkies and all that. He’s got all kinds of 
recipes in the book for making stuff that tastes like all 
those things he hates and fears, but are really just 
soybeans and raw peanut butter. Things like Nut 
Milk, Vegetable Roast, Original Meat (made with 
Gluten, cereal coffee, and peanut meal) and even 
Vegetable Salmon (peanut butter, carrots, tomatoes, and 
water.) Come ON, Jethro! 
If you dig peanut butter 
and really believe its good for you, then get out the soy 
butter and the sugarless jam and the whole wheat bread 
and let the devil take the hindmost. But if meat’s on 
your mind, then tie into some prime rib to lay the ghost, 
and lave your conscience with black cohosh tea and a 
high colonic. What good is it to eat some horrid 
porridge, all the time telling yourself that it’s just 
as good or tastes even better than filet mignon. 
Ohsawa tells you that you'll learn to love the taste 
of brown rice like an old friend, that’s where it’s at. 
Jethro is also very big on baths, compresses, and 
enemas, Having never been an enema freak, | don’t 
really know what kind of a hit you get from them, but 
have never been all that curious, Jethro tells us that if 
you’re not taking two or three shits a day, you’re con- 
stipated, and that a couple of herbal enemas a week is 
very good for you. The spectacle of all those grim 
Christian folk sitting around in lowa shooting water 
up each other's asses is not really conducive to feel- 
ings of lightness and joy. 
Most important, it seems to me that this is a book for 
people who are sick or who are afraid they’re get- 
ting sick. It’s like those dim little health food stores 
which are run by old people who wear druggist’s outfits, 
talk in hushed voices, and sell you vitamin pills, | 
prefer the nice bright open ones that sell groovy tasting 
food, 
It’s basically a book for people who eat health 
foods the way they take medicine, not because they like 
them, but because they’re good for them. People who 
would dart right back to the Safeway and buy all the 
plastic TV dinners if someone proved to them that they 
were harmless and nourishing. People who hate to 
eat, really, but are too afraid of dying not to, 
| really think there must be better books than this one, 
somewhere. Why not ask the Whole Earth readers? 
Love, 
Roland
