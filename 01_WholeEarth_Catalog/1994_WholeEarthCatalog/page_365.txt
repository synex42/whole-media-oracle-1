Leaving School in 9th Grade 
Irv Lofberg of Wisconsin writes: 
| clearly remember, in seventh grade, acci- 
dentally enjoying myself while writing a his- 
tory essay. In the school | attended it was 
an absolute taboo to enjoy oneself while 
learning. | knew it was customary to groan 
when the class was assigned to read an en- 
tire chapter for class, but occasionally | 
would get lost in the words and be truly 
fascinated by the subject. Of course, | 
thought there was something grotesquely 
wrong with me. Enjoy learning? This was 
the greatest sin. 
| also remember an assignment we had in 
eighth grade, which was to write a diary by 
a member of the Lewis and Clark expedi- 
tion. Students would go to the podium to 
read, but would leave much of their won- 
derful detail out in fear of being labeled a 
“geek” or a “brain” (creativity was another 
taboo). My diary was long and very de- 
tailed. | felt ashamed reading my work, 
especially when | heard groans from my 
peers because | was taking too long. | 
sat down, wishing | hadn't read it. 
| found myself struggling to express myself 
in school, but also fearing to do so in the 
horror of being pushed out by my peers. 
The only peace | found in school was 
through writing. But this was also taken 
away when the writing was picked apart 
and graded. It stifled my creativity rather 
than helped it to bloom, because | learned 
how to write for the teacher instead of for 
myself. In group assignments it was worse. 
Most of the students said “I don’t care” and 
closed their minds. Obviously, | was only 
learning how to 
suppress and 
mold myself to 
fit appropriately 
into the system. 
My mental and 
physical health 
were fading fast. 
| had to find a 
way Out.

Child’s Work: Nancy Wallace. Holt Associates, 
2269 Massachusetts Ave., Cambridge, MA 02140. 
Nancy Wallace’s homeschooled kids are, from 
her descriptions, extraordinarily gifted, which 
might be why it worked to allow them to do 
what they wanted to do rather than what society 
expected them to do. Her thesis is radical 
— that children’s work of joyful learning and 
unbounded exploratory play is as important 
as adult work, and that, for the most part, 
kids should be left to find their own way.
