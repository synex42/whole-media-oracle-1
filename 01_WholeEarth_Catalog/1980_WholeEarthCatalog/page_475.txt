Mammals and such are hot blooded, meaning they 
make their heat inside their bodies. Reptiles are 
cold blooded, meaning they get their heat from 
the outside environment. When I was in elemen- 
tary school, they taught us that this means that 
reptiles are the same temperature as the air around 
them, but this is not true in real life. It is only 
true in laboratories, where there is no sun. When 
reptiles are in the sun, they can keep their internal 
temperature within a 2 degree area (like 110-112 
F. for lizards) by changing their relationship to 
the sun. 
In the morning they put their bodies 
lengthwise to the sun to catch all the rays they 
can and in the hot afternoon they face it head 
on or go under rocks so they don’t burn up. 
Every movement has something to do with main- 
taining body heat and body heat is maintained. 
But if you put them in a lab lit by fluorescent 
lights and heated by the radiator, they just lie 
there and become room temperature, and lead 
you to lie to young children. 
—Information from Hot Blooded Dinosaurs
