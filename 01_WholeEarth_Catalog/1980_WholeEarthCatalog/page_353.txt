First off, you may not know what a bell is like 
and how it gets rung, so I'll tell you. A bell is 
like a bell and how it gets rung is it’s hanging from 
the axle of a cart wheel. The cart wheel has a 
rope around it in a shallow yo-yo-groove and one 
end of the rope is attached to the wheel, and you 
pull on the other end of the rope, wheel moves, 
axle moves, bell moves, clapper inside bell stays 
still (gravity, ya know), bell hits clapper, ding. 
But all of the above could not happen unless 
cart wheel, axle and bell were all off the ground 
and able to move freely. 
How, you may, if you're insatiably curious, ask, 
did they get off the ground? Well, my father and 
two other men lifted them onto this here triangu- 
lar stand, this stand made up of two triangles and 
cross beams. Let me put it to you like this. Take 
a swing set, shrink it down, ditch the swings, put 
the ends of the bell axle on those two cross pieces 
at the end of the swing set, bell hanging down 
between the triangles, cartwheel on the outside 
of one of the triangles, and you’ve basically got it. 
In this case, the swing set is made of wood and 
about four feet tall, the bell is about a foot and a 
half tall and the wheel is about 2 feet tall. (Am 
I asking you if you care? Iam not. I’m in this for 
my soul, you remember. I missed fully half of 
Ivanhoe because I skipped the descriptions and 
here I am with bells, exactly as fascinating as 
how many drawstrings in your typical rough 
Saxon ensemble, I’m sure.)
