Learning to see. A working artist needs visual 
reference as a tool for inspiration; for seeing visual 
connections not made before; or as models to 
draw from or photograph. 
Inventing images, draw- 
ing constructions out of the blue, is helped if 
you've got a few aids. 
1 Spy. When / was a kid, one way | learned to see was 
playing “I spy with my little eye, something beginning 
with C’” — “clouds.”” Then, when we got smarter, it 
would be | spy O for “owls in the cloud” and they’d 
have to guess what “O” stood for before the cloud 
blew away. Now / play | spy in my small library of big 
picture books and look out for images and inspiration 
to appear. 
Access. Big picture books, encyclopedias, and reference 
tomes are expensive and often out of print. Since art 
reference is often used as found-art, this is reflected in 
their purchase — a bit of an old encyclopedia is quite 
useful in a found-art context. So | buy my books at 
street sales, the flea market and jumble sales.

Out of copyright material. Copyright images may not 
legally be reproduced photographically but they can often 
be used as reference for drawing from. 
The time period 
for copyright is now the artist’s life plus 50 years, and for 
this reason many 
graphic aids are culled from old sources 
— stuff that’s available to clip or photograph/photostat 
for the taking without a by-your-leave, either because it’s 
real old or published specifically for reproduction.
